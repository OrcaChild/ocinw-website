## Protecting a Hidden Coastal Treasure

Batiquitos Lagoon is one of the last remaining tidal wetlands in Southern California, tucked between the neighborhoods of Carlsbad just off the I-5 corridor. This 610-acre ecological reserve is home to great blue herons, snowy egrets, endangered least terns, and a nursery habitat where young fish grow before heading out to open ocean. The Carlsbad Lagoon Water Quality Watch is Orca Child in the Wild's dedicated monitoring program that tracks the health of this lagoon through hands-on science. Every other Saturday, our volunteer team collects water samples, measures key indicators like pH, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, and temperature, and documents wildlife sightings -- building a long-term dataset that helps protect this irreplaceable ecosystem.

## What Volunteers Do in the Field

Each session starts with a brief training at our meeting point along the Batiquitos Lagoon Trail. Volunteer teams of three to four people rotate through monitoring stations along the lagoon's edge, using portable test kits to measure water quality parameters. Team leads guide newer volunteers through each step -- how to calibrate a pH meter, how to read a turbidity tube, how to record GPS coordinates for each sample site. While water testing is the core activity, volunteers also pick up any litter found along the trail and shoreline, log bird and marine life observations, and photograph the lagoon conditions for our visual record. Sessions run about 90 minutes, and we wrap up with a debrief where teams share anything unusual they noticed -- an algae bloom forming, a new bird species spotted, or water levels that look different from last time.

## The Science Behind the Mission

Water quality monitoring might sound technical, but it tells a powerful story. A drop in dissolved oxygen can signal an algae bloom that suffocates fish. A spike in turbidity after a rainstorm reveals how urban runoff carries pollutants from streets and parking lots into our waterways. By collecting this data consistently over months and years, our volunteers create a picture of lagoon health that no single snapshot could provide. We share our findings with San Diego Coastkeeper and the Batiquitos Lagoon Foundation, who use community-collected data alongside their own research to advocate for stronger stormwater regulations, habitat restoration funding, and development setbacks that protect the lagoon buffer zone. This is real citizen science with real consequences -- the data our youth volunteers gather has the power to influence policy decisions that shape the future of this coastline.

## Why Batiquitos Lagoon Matters

Coastal wetlands like Batiquitos are sometimes called the kidneys of the landscape because they filter pollutants, absorb floodwater, and store carbon. They also serve as critical stopover habitat for migratory birds traveling the Pacific Flyway. Despite their importance, California has lost over 90 percent of its original wetland habitat to development. Every remaining lagoon is precious, and protecting them requires ongoing vigilance. By monitoring water quality and documenting ecological conditions, our volunteers serve as the eyes and ears of this ecosystem -- catching problems early before they become crises.

## Join the Watch Team

We are looking for curious, detail-oriented volunteers who want to learn real field science skills while making a difference. No prior experience is required -- we teach you everything on site. This program is especially great for students interested in marine biology, environmental science, or ecology, and the data collection hours count toward community service and can strengthen college applications. Volunteers under 13 are welcome with a parent or guardian. Bring closed-toe shoes, a hat, sunscreen, and a reusable water bottle. Sign up through our volunteer form and select the Water Quality Watch program. We will send you the next session date, meeting point directions, and everything you need to know to jump in.
